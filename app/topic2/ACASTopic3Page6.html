Summary and Conclusion

You should now be familiar with the fundamentals of ACAS. The system continually tracks other aircraft and, by performing range and altitude tests on them, determines if they are intruding into the protected zone around own aircraft. This protected volume is a function of the sensitivity level and is dependent on altitude. The system determines any evasive action that needs to be taken, decides on the strength and sense required to ensure that an adequate vertical miss distance is achieved, and provides advisories to the crews involved. It can also coordinate the RAs given to aircraft in conflict, assuming TCAS II Version 7.0 systems are carried by the aircraft involved. Finally, it can deal with situations involving multiple threats.

It is important to remember that although the technology is extremely advanced, it is not perfect. It is essential, therefore, that ATC procedures are designed to provide flight safety without any reliance upon the use of ACAS.
