ACAS and TCAS - Concept and Implementation

Increases in air traffic operations have brought with them a greater risk of mid-air collisions between aircraft.

ACAS (Airborne Collision Avoidance System) is the conceptual solution that has been provided to reduce the risk of such accidents. Its accurate use, as a last-resort tool, should ensure greater safety by reducing the risk of mid-air collision between aircraft when other means and methods of separation fail.

ACAS is a generic term or concept philosophy defined by ICAO and standardised in ANNEX10. ACAS is designed to work both autonomously and independently of the aircraft navigation equipment and autopilot systems as well as any ground systems used for the provision of air traffic services. TCAS is the only commercially available implementation of the ICAO standard for ACAS.

Currently, TCAS II is the only implementation that meets the ACAS ICAO Standards and Recommended Practices (SARPs). Therefore, the term ACAS II is typically used when referring to the standard or concept and TCAS II when referring to the implementation. However, often both terms are used interchangeably. ACAS: definitions and concepts

The Airborne Collision Avoidance System (ACAS) concept is an airborne avionics system that acts independently of ground based systems, auto pilot and NAV systems. It acts as a last resort safety net based on secondary surveillance radar (SSR) transponder and Mode S transponder to provide advice to the pilot on potential conflict aircraft that are equipped with the same equipment.

Three types of ACAS have been specified in ICAO Annex 10:

  • ACAS I
  • ACAS II
  • ACAS III
  • measures range and closure rates of other aircraft.
  • alerts pilot when intruder aircraft enters protected volume of airspace and becomes potential threat (Traffic Advisory - TA).
  • provides information as an aid to "see and avoid" action but does not include the capability for generating Resolution Advisory (RAs). ACAS I is not, nor has it ever been, mandated in Europe and there are no operational rules regarding the use of ACAS I.

TCAS implementation: TCAS I

  • measures range and closure rates of other aircraft.
  • alerts pilot when intruder aircraft enters protected volume of airspace and becomes potential threat (Traffic Advisory - TA).
  • provides avoidance manoeuvres for pilots in vertical sense if intruder becomes threat (Resolution Advisory - RA)
  • coordinates and provides complementary RAs (if both aircraft have Mode S transponders

TCAS implementation: TCAS II

  • provides vertical and horizontal RAs in addition to TAs.
  • although ACAS III is mentioned as a future system in ICAO Annex 10, ACAS III is unlikely to materialise due to difficulties the current surveillance systems have with horizontal tracking.
  • currently, research is being conducted to develop a future collision avoidance system under the working name of ACAS X. If developed and certified, ACAS X will not be commercially available before 2020s. It is unclear at this stage whether ACAS X would provide any horizontal resolutions.

TCAS Overview: basics (i)

Traffic Alert and Collision Avoidance System (TCAS II) is an on-board system designed to improve air safety by acting as a 'last-resort' method of preventing mid-air collisions by recommending vertical manoeuvres to pilots when a risk of collision is detected.

Through antennas, TCAS interrogates the !CAO standard compliant transponders of all aircraft in the vicinity. Based upon the replies received, the system tracks the slant range, altitude (when it is included in the reply message) and be.aring of surrounding traffic. The data is passed on to the collision avoidance logic for traffic and resolution advisory selection and display. ( see 'Types of Alert' later in this overview).

TCAS Overview: coverage

The alerts issued by ACAS II depend on the transponder mode of the intruder:

  • There can be no alerts if the transponder is inactive or not compliant with ICAO standards.
  • TAs are issued if the transponder is active and complies with ICAO standards.
  • RA.s are issued if the transponder is altitude reporting and complies with ICAO standards.

Thus the alerts provided for in the ACAS specification are dependent on both the type and the current operational mode of the transponders carried by the airborne aircraft.

In order to maximise the effectiveness of ACAS, a number of regulations have been passed. For example, mandatory carriage of ACAS was first introduced in the USA in 1993. In Europe, the carriage of ACAS II version 7.1 is mandatory from 1 December 2015 by all aircraft currently equipped with version 7.0 and from 1 March 2012 by all new aircraft above 5,700 kg maximum take-off mass or a maximum passenger seating capacity of more than 19. All new ACAS installations after 1 January 2014 shall be compliant with version 7.1 and after 1 January 2017 all ACAS units shall be compliant with version 7.1.
