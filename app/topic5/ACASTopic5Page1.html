Summing up

In situations where ATC has failed to provide separation or a pilot has made a mistake, or in case of system, communication or procedure failure, ACAS II provides an independent safety net to assist in preventing mid-air collisions.It is a reliable, proven and effective system, but its ability to help prevent collisions depends entirely on correct and timely crew response to the collision avoidance manoeuvres - RAs.

It is critically important for flight crews to follow an RA promptly, in the indicated direction, and to the extent displayed by ACAS even if it conflicts with an ATC instruction. Pilots to respond to first RA in 5 seconds, to subsequent RAs within 2,5 seconds and do not manoeuvre contrary to the RA.

It is critically important for ATC to provide separation using the rules and operational procedures prescribed for the area of responsibility concerned. When aware that an aircraft is manoeuvring in response to an RA, the controller must not attempt to modify the aircraft flight path until pilot reports "clear of conflict", but can provide traffic information.

Both ATC and pilots should use correct and standard phraseology.

With regards to the future, experimental tools which can support ACAS II are still under development. One such example was FARADS, the Feasibility of ACAS RA Downlink Study, whose objective was to assess the technical and operational feasibly of displaying ACAS RA information on controllers' working positions (CWPs).

Controllers:

  • Aim for safety separation by rules and procedure.
  • Do not modify the clearances when aware of RA and resume responsibility to provide separation after the event.
  • Provide traffic information.

Pilots:

  • Always follow an ACAS RA even if it conflicts with an ATC clearance or instruction.
  • Never manoeuvre in the opposite sense to an RA.
  • Notify ATC of the RA manoeuvre ASAP (as soon as practical).

For both ATC and pilots:

  • Use correct and standard phraseology.

Circumstances Phraseologies

...after a flight crew starts to deviate from any ATC clearance or instruction to comply with an ACAS resolution advisory (RA) (Pilot and controller interchange):

PILOT: TCAS RA;

ATC: ROGER;

...after the response to an ACAS RA is completed and a return to the ATC clearance or instruction is initiated (Pilot and controller interchange):

PILOT: CLEAR OF CONFLICT, RETURNING TO (assigned clearance);

ATC: ROGER (or alternative instructions);

...after the response to an ACAS RA is completed and the assigned ATC clearance or instruction has been resumed (Pilot and controller interchange):

PILOT: CLEAR OF CONFLICT (assigned clearance) RESUMED;

ATC: ROGER (or alternative instructions);

...after an ATC clearance or instruction contradictory to the ACAS RA is received, the flight crew will follow the RA and inform ATC directly (Pilot and controller interchange):

PILOT: UNABLE, TCAS RA;

ATC: ROGER;

FARADS

The aims of these experiments were twofold. Firstly, to display RAs to controllers on the radar display, and secondly, to measure and obtain feedback on the effects of this on their situational awareness, working methods, and capability of handling traffic in situations where an RA is issued. The RA downlink work continued through the PASS (Performance and Safety Aspects of STCA, full Study). The PASS project's objective was to study performance and safety aspects of Short Term Conflict Alert (STCA), including human performance aspects and consideration of interactions between operational use of STCA and ACAS.

RA downlink research is now progressed in the following SESAR (Single European Sky ATM Research) projects:

  • Project 4.8.3 - Ground-Airborne Safety Net Compatibility (operational aspects)
  • Project 15.4.3 - ACAS Monitoring

If proved feasible and safe:

  • RA Downlink will provide an automatic notification to the controller about RAs generated in the cockpit.
  • Whereas the controller might, for whatever reason, miss the verbal RA report from pilots, RA Downlink information will remain on the controller screen until Clear of Conflict.
  • RA Downlink will be more dependable, more structured and often faster than current voice communications.

Potential benefits of RA Downlink include:

  • Reduced likelihood of contradictory clearances.
  • Improved situational awareness.
  • More up-to-date traffic information, especially to other aircraft in the vicinity.
  • Better post-conflict traffic planning.

For further info visit website: www.eurocontrol.int/ra-downlink

ACAS X

  • Overview
  • Offline development
  • Real-time operation

ACAS X is based on a probabilistic model providing a statistical representation of the aircraft position in the future. It also takes into account the safety and operational objectives of the system enabling the logic to be tailored to particular procedures or airspace configurations.

This is fed into an optimisation process called dynamic programming to determine the best course of action to follow according to the context of the conflict. This takes account of a rewards versus costs system to determine which action would generate the greatest benefits (i.e. maintain a safe separation whiIe implementing a cost-effective avoidance manoeuvre). Key metrics for operational suitability and pilot acceptability include minimizing the frequency of alerts that result in reversals/intentional intruder altitude crossings or disruptive advisories in noncritical encounters.

The lookup table is used in real-time on-board the aircraft to resolve conflicts. ACAS X collects surveillance measurements from an array of sources (approximately every second). Various models are used (e.g. a probabilistic sensor model accounting for sensor error characteristics) to estimate an state distribution, which is a probability distribution over the current positions and velocities of the aircraft. The state distribution determines where to look in the numeric lookup table to determine the best action to take. If deemed necessary, resolution advisories are then issued to pilots.
